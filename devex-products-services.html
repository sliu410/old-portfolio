Redesigning the Devex membership
and services page

UX design | front-end development


The project is to redesign the marketing and services page, which lists a variety of products and services of Devex. It is the main entrance point that can lead to all products’ marketing pages, so redesigning the page is of huge significance. I worked closely with a PM on this project and led interaction design and front-end development.

Problems and solutions


I cannot show you the original version of the webpage, but I can list some of the previous problems and how I tackled them.

Problem 1: The original page had four categories of products and they were listed in six rows from top to bottom. This layout unintentionally delivered a sense of hierarchy within the products. Also, the products listed at the bottom often got neglected.

Solution 1: We decided to arrange all products in one row, so users can choose their desired products at first glance. If a user wants to learn more about a certain category, by clicking “learn more” he/she can see the unfolding part that gives specific information. After chatting with marketing professionals, we did recognize that within each category some of the products were worth more attention, so we differentiated the “star products” by placing them in prominent places and coloring the icons and buttons.

Problem 2: The second problem was actually a setback of the first solution. In the new design, users needed one more step—clicking “learn more”—to go to a specific product. This is the trade-off we were facing.

Solution 2: Under the first-level title we created four quick links that can lead users to the specific services immediately. Since the top priority of the webpage was to lead users to each product's or service’s marketing page, so we made sure the four quick links were not stealing the limelight but also were convenient for returned visitors.

Problem 3: It was not easy to choose an appropriate and simplistic background image for the above-the-fold section. With the variety of products and services, no single image can represent the inclusive webpage.

Solution 3: Since it was impossible to find a single photo, I decided to go for something abstract but sleek. I blurred an image and toned down its brightness. The contrast can also direct more attention to the title and the four main product categories. I also tried using white or solid-color background, but the visual result was not as good as a blurred image.

Problem 4: After I finished coding the webpage, we tested the mockups with several users and found a major problem that was not uncovered in the static version. The four “learn-more” buttons were close to the bottom edge of the screen, so users would not find the unfolding part that was below the buttons.

Solution 4: I used the scrollTop function in javascript to make the unfolding part scroll to top of the screen. This simple function makes the user experience much more pleasant :)

Reflections


The major takeaway from this project is that we designers need to balance the pros and cons of each design. When confronted with trade-offs, we need to figure out ways to diminish most inconvenience in user experience. Also, polishing the final deliverable is very important. Some problems, especially those tiny ones, will not surface unless you test the final products.
